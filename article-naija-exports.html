Beyond Oil: The Nigerian Products the World Is Buying in 2026

Nigeria's non-oil exports are growing. Here are the products and entrepreneurs building an economy that goes beyond crude oil.

✍ NaijaBuzz Editorial•May 2026•6 min read

For most of its post-independence history, Nigeria has been a one-product economy — the price of crude oil determining the state of government finances, the strength of the naira, and the general mood of the business environment. This dependency has made Nigeria extraordinarily vulnerable to a commodity whose price it cannot control and whose long-term demand trajectory, in an era of accelerating energy transition, is uncertain. But in 2026, a quiet diversification is underway, driven not primarily by government policy but by entrepreneurs, farmers, and creative industries finding global markets for Nigerian products.

Agricultural exports are leading the non-oil growth story. Sesame seeds — primarily grown in Jigawa, Borno, and Nassarawa states — have become Nigeria's largest non-oil agricultural export, with annual export values exceeding $600 million. Nigerian sesame is particularly prized in Asian markets for oil extraction and food processing. Cashew nuts from Oyo, Kogi, and other states contribute significantly to export earnings, with global demand growing faster than Nigerian production can currently supply. And cocoa — Nigeria's historic export crop that was displaced by oil — is experiencing a renaissance, with demand from European chocolate manufacturers remaining strong and premium for certified sustainable Nigerian cocoa growing.

The digital creative economy represents a newer but rapidly growing export sector. Nigerian music — Afrobeats in all its subgenres — generates streaming royalties, touring income, and merchandise revenue from global audiences that constitute an invisible but real export of cultural product. Nigerian film — Nollywood content licensed to Netflix and other streaming platforms — generates foreign exchange in the form of licensing fees. Nigerian tech products, including software built by Nigerian developers for global markets, contribute to service exports that the national accounts barely capture.

Fashion and textile exports are small but growing. Nigerian designers selling internationally through e-commerce platforms, and fabric producers exporting Ankara, Aso-oke, and Adire to diaspora markets in the UK, US, and elsewhere, are building genuine export businesses. The global appetite for authentic African textiles — driven by diaspora communities and international consumers seeking cultural authenticity — is a market that Nigerian producers are beginning to serve more systematically.

For Nigerian entrepreneurs, the lesson is clear: the global market for Nigerian products — agricultural, creative, and digital — exists and is growing. The barriers to accessing it have never been lower, with e-commerce platforms, digital payment systems, and international logistics networks making export possible for businesses of almost any size. The Nigerian brand — associated with creativity, entrepreneurialism, and cultural richness — is an asset that international buyers increasingly respond to positively. Building beyond oil is not a government project. It is an entrepreneurial opportunity waiting to be seized.

🔑 In Practice: Nigeria's non-oil export economy is real and growing. If you produce quality goods or services, global markets are more accessible than ever through e-commerce and digital platforms.
