Do You Really Need a University Degree in Nigeria in 2026? The Honest Answer

With ASUU strikes, rising tuition, and changing job markets, many Nigerians are asking whether a university degree is still worth it.

✍ NaijaBuzz Editorial•May 2026•7 min read

For generations of Nigerians, the pathway to a decent life was clear and non-negotiable: finish secondary school, pass JAMB, enter university, graduate with a degree, and find a good job. In 2026, that pathway is increasingly being questioned — not because education has become less important, but because the relationship between formal degrees and economic success has become far more complicated than it once was.

The evidence that degree-holders are struggling is visible everywhere. Hundreds of thousands of Nigerian graduates compete annually for a shrinking pool of formal sector jobs that pay salaries that have been decimated by inflation. Meanwhile, plumbers, electricians, welders, and skilled tradespeople — many without university degrees — are earning incomes that comfortably exceed those of graduate professionals in banking or the civil service. A skilled electrician in Lagos charges ₦30,000 to ₦80,000 per job and completes multiple jobs per week. A fresh banking graduate earns ₦80,000 per month.

In the technology sector, the degree question has been largely settled by the market. The largest tech companies hiring Nigerian talent — both locally and for remote roles — evaluate candidates on demonstrable skills, portfolio work, and problem-solving ability rather than certificates. A self-taught developer with a strong GitHub portfolio and two years of project experience will consistently outcompete a computer science graduate whose degree-level coding skills have not been applied in practical projects.

Vocational and technical education is experiencing a quiet renaissance in Nigeria. Institutions like the Industrial Training Fund and a growing number of private vocational academies are producing graduates in plumbing, electrical engineering, welding, automotive repair, and construction management who are immediately employable at strong wages. The stigma that has historically attached to vocational training in Nigeria — driven by the cultural prestige of white-collar work — is slowly eroding as the economic reality becomes impossible to ignore.

The honest answer to whether you need a university degree in Nigeria in 2026 is: it depends on what you want to do. Medicine, law, architecture, and engineering still require formal degrees and professional licensing that cannot be bypassed. But in technology, business, creative industries, and the trades, demonstrable skill and consistent performance matter far more than the certificate on your wall. The most valuable education in 2026 — with or without a degree — is one that produces skills the market actually needs.

📍 In Practice: A university degree remains valuable in Nigeria but is no longer the only path. Vocational training, tech bootcamps, and professional certifications are creating real alternatives for motivated people.
