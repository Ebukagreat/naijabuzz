The Business of Nigerian Street Food: How Suya, Bole, and Akara Sellers Make Millions

The suya seller at your junction may be earning more than many office workers. Here is the business of Nigerian street food.

✍ NaijaBuzz Staff•May 2026•5 min read

Look carefully at the suya seller at the corner of a busy Lagos street on a Friday evening. Watch the volume of transactions — ₦2,000 here, ₦3,500 there, a ₦10,000 order for a group heading to a party. Calculate the number of customers served between 5pm and 10pm. The arithmetic, for a well-positioned suya seller in a busy neighbourhood, frequently works out to ₦50,000 to ₦150,000 in a single evening. Monthly, a serious suya operation can gross ₦1.5 million to ₦4 million. After the cost of meat, spices, charcoal, and labour, net margins of 30 to 40% are achievable. This is not a supplementary income. It is a serious business.

Nigerian street food occupies a unique economic position. The product has universal demand — Nigerians across all income levels eat street food regularly, driven by convenience, affordability, and the genuine quality of traditional preparations that no fast food chain has successfully replicated. The startup costs are relatively modest compared to formal restaurant businesses — a suya setup requires a coal grill, a good knife, reliable meat supply, and the spice blend that is the seller's proprietary competitive advantage. And the cash flow is immediate — unlike most businesses, street food vendors collect payment at the point of sale with no credit risk.

The bole and fish business, particularly popular in Rivers State and spreading rapidly to other urban centres, is another street food category generating serious income. A bole seller in Port Harcourt with a good location and consistent quality can serve 200 to 400 customers daily, each spending between ₦500 and ₦2,000. The raw materials — plantain and fresh fish — are affordable and available. The preparation requires skill but not formal training. And the product commands intense customer loyalty — bole buyers return to the same seller repeatedly because quality and consistency matter enormously in this category.

Akara — deep-fried bean cakes — and moi moi sellers represent the breakfast end of the street food economy. In any busy Nigerian neighbourhood, the akara seller who sets up by 6am and maintains consistent quality will build a loyal customer base within weeks. The economics are compelling: a bag of beans costing ₦8,000 produces akara that sells for ₦25,000 to ₦35,000 in retail portions. The skill is in the preparation — the right grind, the right seasoning, the right frying temperature — and in the consistency that keeps customers coming back.

What separates the street food sellers who build serious wealth from those who merely survive is business discipline. Tracking costs and revenue. Maintaining quality standards regardless of ingredient price fluctuations. Building a loyal customer base through consistency rather than chasing volume at the expense of quality. And reinvesting profits into better equipment, better locations, or additional product lines rather than consuming every naira earned. The street food business has made millionaires in Nigeria. It will continue to do so for anyone who treats it with the seriousness it deserves.

💡 Key Takeaway: Nigerian street food is a serious business with low startup costs, high demand, and strong margins. If you have culinary skills and business discipline, it is one of Nigeria's most accessible wealth-building opportunities.
