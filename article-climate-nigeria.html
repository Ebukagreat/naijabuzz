Climate Change and Nigeria: How Rising Temperatures Are Already Affecting Everyday Life

Nigeria is one of the most climate-vulnerable countries in the world. Here is how climate change is already reshaping daily Nigerian life.

✍ NaijaBuzz Reporters•May 2026•6 min read

For many Nigerians, climate change is an abstract concept — something discussed at international conferences by world leaders, affecting polar ice caps and distant island nations, but not immediately relevant to the daily reality of Lagos traffic, Abuja bureaucracy, or farming in Kano. This perception is dangerously incorrect. Nigeria is one of the countries most acutely affected by climate change in the world today, and the impacts are already visible, measurable, and accelerating across every region of the country.

In northern Nigeria, the advance of the Sahara Desert — desertification driven by reduced rainfall, overgrazing, and rising temperatures — has claimed millions of hectares of previously productive farmland over the past three decades. The Lake Chad basin, which once supported the livelihoods of tens of millions of people across four countries, has shrunk by 90% since 1960 due to a combination of climate change and water extraction. The displacement of farming and fishing communities by this environmental collapse has contributed directly to resource conflicts, food insecurity, and the recruitment conditions that have fuelled insurgencies in the northeast.

In southern Nigeria, the pattern of extreme rainfall events has become more intense and less predictable. The flooding that devastated communities in Anambra, Delta, Rivers, and Bayelsa states in recent years — inundating homes, destroying crops, displacing hundreds of thousands of people — has been directly linked to altered rainfall patterns consistent with climate change projections. Coastal erosion, driven by rising sea levels and increased wave energy, is claiming land in Lagos and Delta State at rates that threaten communities and infrastructure.

The agricultural consequences are already visible in Nigerian food prices. Staple crops including maize, sorghum, and millet are experiencing yield declines in the Middle Belt as rainfall patterns become less reliable. Cocoa production in the southwest is being affected by changing temperature and humidity regimes that alter pest and disease pressure on plantations. And the seasonal cycles that farmers have relied upon for generations are becoming less predictable, making planting and harvesting decisions more uncertain and crop failures more common.

For individual Nigerians, climate adaptation is not a government responsibility alone. Farmers can adopt climate-resilient practices — drought-tolerant crop varieties, water harvesting techniques, agroforestry — that reduce vulnerability to rainfall variability. Urban households can invest in water storage and drainage management that reduces flood risk. Communities can plant trees and restore vegetation that reduces erosion and moderates local temperature. And all Nigerians can support the political and policy changes needed to reduce deforestation, expand renewable energy, and build the infrastructure resilience that a changing climate demands. Nigeria did not cause climate change. But it is among the countries that will suffer most from it — which makes responding to it not an option but a necessity.

📍 What To Do With This: Climate change is already affecting Nigerian food prices, flooding patterns, and agricultural productivity. Adapt now by diversifying income sources, investing in water storage, and supporting climate-resilient farming.
