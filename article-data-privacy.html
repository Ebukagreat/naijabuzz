Your Data Is Being Collected in Nigeria: What You Need to Know and How to Protect Yourself

Every app on your phone is collecting data about you. Here is what Nigerian consumers need to know about data privacy in 2026.

✍ NaijaBuzz Desk•May 2026•5 min read

Every time you open a Nigerian fintech app, scroll through an e-commerce platform, use a food delivery service, or interact with a social media platform, data about you is being collected, stored, and in many cases monetised. Your location, your purchasing behaviour, your browsing history, your contact list, your financial transactions, and your biometric data — the fingerprint or face scan you use to unlock your banking app — are all valuable commodities in a global data economy that most Nigerian consumers participate in without fully understanding the terms.

Nigeria passed the Nigeria Data Protection Act in 2023, bringing the country's data protection framework broadly into alignment with international standards. The Act requires organisations that collect personal data to obtain informed consent, to use data only for the purposes stated at collection, to protect data from unauthorised access, and to allow individuals to request deletion of their data. The Nigeria Data Protection Commission is responsible for enforcement. In practice, however, awareness of these rights among Nigerian consumers remains low, and enforcement against the most common privacy violations is still developing.

The most immediate privacy risks for ordinary Nigerians come from three sources. First, over-permissioned apps — applications that request access to contacts, location, microphone, camera, and storage far beyond what their stated function requires. A flashlight app that requests access to your contact list is not using that access for your benefit. Reviewing and revoking unnecessary app permissions — available in your phone's settings under each app — is the most impactful privacy action most Nigerians can take immediately. Second, weak and reused passwords. Using the same password across multiple accounts means that a single data breach — which happen regularly at even large Nigerian companies — compromises every account that shares that password. Third, phishing — fake websites and communications designed to steal login credentials — which exploits the trust established by legitimate brands.

Practical privacy protection does not require technical expertise. Using a password manager — applications like Bitwarden, which is free, generate and store unique strong passwords for every account — addresses the password problem entirely. Enabling two-factor authentication on every important account — banking, email, social media — means that stolen passwords alone are insufficient to access your accounts. Being deliberately cautious about which apps you install and what permissions you grant is a habit that takes seconds to implement and provides continuous protection.

The broader principle worth internalising is that in the digital economy, if a product or service is genuinely free, the product is often you — specifically your data and attention. This is not necessarily sinister, but it is worth understanding clearly. Nigerian consumers who understand what they are exchanging for free services, who read privacy policies before installing apps, and who exercise their legal rights to access and delete their data are better positioned in an economy that is increasingly built on the commodification of personal information.

📍 The Real Lesson: Review app permissions on your phone, use a strong unique password for each important account, enable two-factor authentication everywhere, and read privacy policies before installing new apps.
