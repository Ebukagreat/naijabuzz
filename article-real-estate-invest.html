How to Invest in Nigerian Real Estate Without Buying a House

Nigerian property prices are out of reach for most people. But there are ways to invest in real estate without buying a house.

✍ NaijaBuzz Staff•May 2026•6 min read

Nigerian real estate has historically been one of the most reliable wealth-building assets in the country, delivering consistent appreciation that has outpaced inflation and most financial instruments over the long term. A plot of land in Lekki purchased for ₦500,000 in 2005 is worth over ₦50 million today. An apartment in Ikeja bought for ₦8 million in 2010 now sells for over ₦60 million. The challenge is that entry costs have risen to the point where direct property ownership is beyond the financial reach of most Nigerians — particularly the young, urban professionals who most need to build long-term wealth.

Real Estate Investment Trusts — REITs — are the most accessible and regulated entry point into Nigerian real estate for small investors. REITs are companies that own income-producing real estate — office buildings, shopping malls, hotels, and residential developments — and are legally required to distribute at least 90% of their taxable income to shareholders as dividends. In Nigeria, UPDC REIT and SFS Real Estate Investment Trust are listed on the Nigerian Exchange and can be purchased through any stockbroker for as little as ₦5,000. Investors receive regular dividend income and participate in property value appreciation without the management responsibilities of direct ownership.

Real estate crowdfunding platforms have emerged as a popular alternative for Nigerians seeking more direct property exposure. Platforms like Rise Vest Real Estate, Coreum, and several others allow investors to pool funds with other investors to purchase specific properties — residential developments, commercial spaces, or short-let apartments — with minimum investments as low as ₦50,000 to ₦100,000. Returns are generated through rental income and property appreciation, distributed proportionally to investors.

Cooperative housing schemes — both government-sponsored and private — remain one of the most underutilised pathways to property ownership for salaried Nigerians. The Federal Mortgage Bank of Nigeria's National Housing Fund scheme allows contributors to access mortgage loans at below-market interest rates for properties they could not otherwise afford. Many state governments run similar schemes. The key requirement is consistent monthly contributions over an extended period — a discipline that rewards those who start early.

Short-let property management is another avenue for Nigerians who have some capital but not enough for direct purchase. Some entrepreneurs partner with property owners to manage their apartments as short-let accommodation on platforms like Airbnb and local equivalents, earning a management fee of 20 to 30% of revenue without owning the property outright. This model builds real estate management experience and income while accumulating capital toward eventual ownership. The Nigerian property market rewards patience and creativity in equal measure.

💡 Key Takeaway: REITs, real estate crowdfunding platforms, and cooperative housing schemes allow Nigerians to invest in property with small amounts. Start with what you can afford and build gradually.
