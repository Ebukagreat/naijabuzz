Your Pension in Nigeria: What You Need to Know to Retire Comfortably

Most Nigerians will reach retirement with almost nothing saved. Here is how to use the pension system and additional strategies to retire comfortably.

✍ NaijaBuzz Staff•May 2026•6 min read

Retirement is the financial event that most Nigerians plan for least and feel the consequences of most acutely. In a country where extended family obligations mean that children are expected to support elderly parents, and where the public pension system has a history of delayed and denied payments, the prospect of a dignified retirement — one in which a person can meet their own needs without depending entirely on their children — requires deliberate planning that most Nigerian workers never undertake.

The Contributory Pension Scheme, introduced in 2004 under the Pension Reform Act, is the foundation of retirement savings for formal sector Nigerian workers. Under this system, employees contribute a minimum of 8% of their monthly emolument to an individual Retirement Savings Account, with employers contributing a minimum of 10%. These contributions are managed by licensed Pension Fund Administrators — companies like ARM Pensions, Stanbic IBTC Pensions, and Access Pensions — who invest the funds in approved financial instruments. At retirement — defined as age 50 or above — the accumulated balance can be accessed through a programmed withdrawal or annuity arrangement.

The most important thing every formal sector Nigerian worker should do immediately is verify that their employer is actually remitting pension contributions on their behalf. Employer non-remittance — paying the employee salary but failing to remit pension deductions to the PFA — is a common problem in Nigeria, particularly in smaller private companies. You can check your RSA balance on your PFA's app or website, or through the National Pension Commission's online portal. If contributions are missing, reporting to PENCOM initiates an investigation and recovery process.

The mandatory pension contribution alone is almost certainly insufficient for a comfortable retirement. The replacement rate — the proportion of pre-retirement income that pension savings typically provide — under the current system is estimated at 25 to 40% for most workers, meaning retirees can expect their pension income to cover only a quarter to two-fifths of their working income. Bridging this gap requires voluntary additional contributions to the RSA — which also attract tax deductions — and additional retirement savings through instruments like mutual funds, treasury bills, and real estate that operate outside the pension system.

Starting early is the single most powerful retirement planning decision available to young Nigerians. The mathematical impact of compound returns means that ₦10,000 invested monthly from age 25 produces dramatically more retirement wealth than ₦20,000 invested monthly from age 40 — even though the later investor contributes more total money. Time in the market, not the size of individual contributions, is the primary driver of retirement wealth accumulation. Every year of delay is not a neutral decision — it is a costly one whose price is paid at retirement.

💡 Key Takeaway: Check your RSA balance regularly on the PENCOM portal, make voluntary contributions above the mandatory minimum, and start additional retirement savings outside the pension system as early as possible.
