Maternal Health in Nigeria: Why Childbirth Is Still Dangerous and What Every Woman Needs to Know

Nigeria accounts for over 20% of global maternal deaths. Every woman in Nigeria deserves to know the facts and how to protect herself.

✍ NaijaBuzz Staff•May 2026•6 min read

Nigeria accounts for more than 20% of global maternal deaths despite representing less than 3% of the world's population. With approximately 512 maternal deaths per 100,000 live births, Nigeria's maternal mortality ratio is among the highest in the world — a statistic that represents not just a healthcare failure but a profound injustice visited on Nigerian women and their families every single day. Understanding the causes, the risk factors, and the available protections is information that every Nigerian woman of reproductive age deserves to have.

The major direct causes of maternal death in Nigeria are postpartum haemorrhage — excessive bleeding after childbirth — which accounts for approximately 23% of maternal deaths, followed by hypertensive disorders of pregnancy including pre-eclampsia and eclampsia, sepsis from infection, unsafe abortion, and obstructed labour. What makes these causes so tragic is that all of them are preventable or manageable with appropriate skilled care — the same conditions that are routinely managed without fatality in adequately resourced healthcare facilities worldwide.

The indirect causes — the reasons Nigerian women are disproportionately vulnerable — are structural. Geographic barriers mean that many women in rural Nigeria must travel hours to reach a facility with a skilled birth attendant. Financial barriers prevent women from accessing even basic antenatal care. The chronic shortage of doctors, midwives, and nurses means that facilities are understaffed and unable to provide the monitoring and intervention that complicated deliveries require. And cultural factors — including the preference for traditional birth attendants in some communities, and the minimisation of obstetric warning signs — delay care-seeking until emergencies are beyond management.

The practical information that every Nigerian woman needs begins with antenatal care. The World Health Organization recommends a minimum of eight antenatal contacts during pregnancy, starting in the first trimester. Each visit provides an opportunity to screen for conditions that increase risk — anaemia, hypertension, gestational diabetes, malpresentation — and to intervene before they become emergencies. Many of the conditions that kill Nigerian women are detectable and manageable when identified early. The women who die are disproportionately those who receive no antenatal care or begin it too late.

Every pregnant Nigerian woman and her family should know the danger signs that require immediate emergency care: severe headache with visual disturbance or swelling of the face and hands, which may indicate pre-eclampsia. Heavy vaginal bleeding at any stage of pregnancy. Fever with abdominal pain. Reduced or absent fetal movement after 28 weeks. Prolonged labour without progress. Convulsions. These are emergencies that cannot wait. The decision to seek care immediately rather than waiting to see if things improve is one of the most important decisions a pregnant woman and her family can make.

💡 Key Takeaway: Antenatal care from the first trimester, delivery in a facility with a skilled birth attendant, and knowing the danger signs of obstetric emergencies are the three most important factors in safe motherhood in Nigeria.
