Why Nigeria Is Losing Its Doctors and What It Means for Your Healthcare

Nigeria has one doctor for every 5,000 people — far below WHO recommendations. And thousands more are leaving every year.

✍ NaijaBuzz Staff•May 2026•6 min read

Nigeria trains approximately 5,000 medical doctors annually across its medical schools. In a country of 220 million people with one of the highest disease burdens in the world, this production rate is already woefully inadequate. But the true state of Nigeria's doctor workforce is significantly worse than the training numbers suggest, because a substantial proportion of every graduating class leaves the country within two to five years of qualification. The Nigerian Medical Association estimates that over 16,000 Nigerian doctors are currently working in the United Kingdom alone — more than the total number of doctors currently registered and practising in several Nigerian states.

The drivers of this exodus are straightforward and difficult to argue with on an individual level. A Nigerian doctor in a government hospital earns between ₦200,000 and ₦400,000 monthly — the equivalent of $130 to $260 at current exchange rates. The same doctor, after passing the PLAB examination and securing a position in the UK National Health Service, earns between £35,000 and £60,000 annually — a 15 to 25 times increase in real income. Nigerian hospitals frequently lack basic supplies, equipment, and drugs, making it impossible to practice medicine to the standard the doctor was trained for. And the physical safety and infrastructure conditions in many Nigerian hospitals are simply incompatible with professional dignity.

The consequences for Nigerian patients are severe and worsening. The WHO recommends a minimum of one doctor per 1,000 people; Nigeria's ratio is approximately one per 5,500 and deteriorating. Public hospitals that are understaffed run waiting times that stretch to days for specialist consultations. Preventable deaths from conditions that would be routinely managed in adequately staffed facilities occur with alarming frequency. And the burden falls most heavily on those who cannot afford the private healthcare sector — the rural poor, the urban working class, the elderly.

The systemic response requires sustained, serious investment in healthcare funding, doctor remuneration, hospital infrastructure, and the supply chains that keep facilities functional. These are not new recommendations — they have been made by medical associations, civil society organisations, and international health bodies for decades. The political will to implement them has been the missing variable. In the meantime, the individual response that every Nigerian can make is to invest in preventive health — maintaining a healthy weight, monitoring blood pressure and blood sugar regularly, attending annual health screenings, and treating chronic conditions consistently — reducing dependence on a system that is structurally unable to cope with the demand placed upon it.

💡 Key Takeaway: Invest in your preventive health now — regular checkups, blood pressure monitoring, blood sugar testing, and healthy lifestyle habits — because the healthcare system cannot absorb the burden of preventable illness.
