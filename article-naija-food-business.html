How to Start a Catering or Food Business in Nigeria and Make It Profitable

Food is Nigeria's most recession-proof business. Here is how to start a catering or food business and make it genuinely profitable.

✍ NaijaBuzz Desk•May 2026•7 min read

Food is the most recession-proof business in Nigeria. Regardless of economic conditions — inflation, devaluation, political instability — Nigerians eat. They eat at home, at work, at parties, at funerals, at naming ceremonies, and increasingly through delivery services that have brought restaurant-quality meals to the doorstep. The food business encompasses everything from a single woman selling jollof rice from a pot at a bus stop to a catering company serving 2,000 guests at a wedding. Somewhere in that range is an entry point accessible to almost anyone with culinary skill, business discipline, and the willingness to start.

The most important decision in starting a Nigerian food business is choosing a focus. The most common mistake is attempting to offer everything — jollof rice, fried rice, pepper soup, egusi, puff puff, small chops — before establishing excellence in any single item. The food businesses that build loyal customer bases and strong word-of-mouth fastest are those that become known for doing one or two things extraordinarily well. The best jollof rice in the neighbourhood. The freshest pepper soup in the market. The most consistently good small chops at every event. Specialisation builds reputation, and reputation builds a sustainable business.

The economics of a home-based catering business make it one of the most accessible entry points for entrepreneurs with limited capital. A basic setup — quality pots, a gas cooker and cylinder, serving equipment, and initial ingredients — can be assembled for between ₦50,000 and ₦150,000. Operating from home eliminates rent as an expense. WhatsApp is the primary sales and marketing channel — a well-curated WhatsApp Business profile with food photos, pricing, and customer testimonials is more effective than most formal marketing for this type of business. And the Nigerian social calendar — with its constant stream of birthdays, weddings, naming ceremonies, and office events — provides a consistent demand pipeline for caterers who build a reputation for reliability and quality.

Pricing is where many Nigerian food businesses undermine themselves. The instinct to price low to attract customers produces a business that is busy but unprofitable — selling food at prices that do not cover the true cost of ingredients, time, gas, transportation, and packaging. Calculating the full cost of each meal or catering package — including a realistic value for your own time — and pricing above that cost is not optional. It is the difference between a business and an expensive hobby. Nigerian consumers who value quality will pay fair prices for it; those who will only buy at unsustainably low prices are not the customers that build a profitable business.

The growth path for a successful Nigerian food business follows a recognisable pattern. Start home-based and local, building a reputation through consistent quality and excellent customer service. Use social media — particularly Instagram and WhatsApp — to document and share your food visually, as appetite is largely driven by sight. Expand into catering for events as your reputation grows, as event catering delivers higher revenue per transaction than daily meal service. And when volume justifies it, consider a physical location — a small canteen, a cloud kitchen, or a food stall — that provides a more formal base for expansion. The Nigerian food business has built more millionaires than most people realise. It starts with a pot, a recipe, and the discipline to execute both consistently.

💡 The Honest Summary: Start with one signature dish, serve it consistently and excellently, build your customer base through word of mouth and WhatsApp, and reinvest every profit before spending. The Nigerian food business rewards consistency above all else.
