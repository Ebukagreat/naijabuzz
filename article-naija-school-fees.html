How Nigerian Parents Can Afford Private School Fees Without Destroying Their Finances

Nigerian private school fees are rising 20 to 30% annually. Here is how to give your children quality education without destroying your finances.

✍ NaijaBuzz Reporters•May 2026•6 min read

The cost of private education in Nigeria has become one of the most significant financial burdens facing Nigerian middle-class families — and one of the most poorly planned for. Annual fees at mid-range private primary and secondary schools in Lagos, Abuja, and Port Harcourt currently range from ₦500,000 to ₦3 million, with elite schools charging ₦5 million to ₦15 million annually. These figures represent 50 to 200% of the annual salary of many of the parents paying them — a financial commitment that, without deliberate planning, can consume retirement savings, eliminate emergency funds, and leave families genuinely financially vulnerable.

The school fee problem is primarily a planning problem rather than an income problem. Most Nigerian parents make school choice decisions based on aspiration and social comparison — choosing schools whose fees are at the limit of what they can currently afford, without accounting for annual fee increases that typically run at 20 to 30%, or the additional costs of uniforms, textbooks, excursions, and levies that add 20 to 40% to the headline term fees. A school that costs ₦600,000 annually when a child starts primary one will cost ₦2.5 million or more by the time that child reaches SS3 — a trajectory that most parents have not modelled when making their initial school choice.

The most effective financial response to school fees is starting a dedicated school fees savings plan as early as possible — ideally before the child begins school, and certainly at least one year before each academic year. Setting aside a fixed monthly amount into a locked savings account or short-term investment — a 12-month treasury bill or fixed deposit that matures in time for school fees payment — removes the cash flow shock of large termly fee payments and earns interest on the accumulated funds. Parents who implement this discipline find that school fees become manageable rather than crisis-inducing.

Researching the full range of quality schools at different price points before committing to enrollment is equally important. The assumption that fees correlate directly with educational quality in Nigeria is not consistently supported by evidence. Many excellent schools charge moderate fees; many expensive schools charge primarily for prestige, facilities, and social positioning rather than demonstrably superior academic outcomes. Visiting multiple schools at different price points, speaking with parents whose children have attended the schools, reviewing academic performance records, and assessing the quality of teaching staff rather than only facilities produces better school choices than simply selecting the most expensive option that income can theoretically support.

The financial boundaries that must be protected even under school fee pressure are non-negotiable for long-term family financial health. Retirement savings — whether through pension contributions or independent investment — must not be sacrificed for school fees. The child's education is important, but a parent who reaches retirement with nothing saved becomes financially dependent on that same child in their most productive adult years. Emergency funds — three to six months of living expenses held in liquid savings — must not be depleted for school fees, because the emergencies that funds are held against — medical crises, job loss, urgent repairs — do not respect academic calendars. Education is a priority. It cannot be the only priority.

📍 What This Means For You: Start a dedicated school fees savings plan at least one year ahead, research schools at every price point before committing, and never compromise your retirement savings or emergency fund to pay school fees.
