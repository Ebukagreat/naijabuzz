Music Copyright in Nigeria: What Every Artist Needs to Know to Protect Their Work

Nigerian artists create some of the world's most copied music yet earn a fraction of what they are owed. Here is how copyright protection works.

✍ NaijaBuzz Staff•June 2026•6 min read

Nigeria produces some of the most commercially successful and widely reproduced music in the world, yet Nigerian artists collectively receive only a fraction of the revenue their work generates globally. The gap between the value that Nigerian music creates and the income that Nigerian artists capture from it is partly a function of the structural challenges of the global music industry and partly a function of inadequate copyright protection, poor contract literacy, and underutilisation of the legal and institutional frameworks that do exist to protect creators. Understanding copyright is not optional for serious Nigerian musicians — it is the foundation on which sustainable music careers are built.

Copyright in Nigeria is governed by the Copyright Act, which grants original creators of musical works automatic copyright protection from the moment of creation — no registration is required for copyright to exist. What copyright gives a musician is the exclusive right to reproduce, distribute, perform, broadcast, and create derivative works from their original creation. Anyone who uses the work without permission is infringing copyright and is liable for damages. The challenge in Nigeria is not the absence of legal protection but the inadequate enforcement of protections that theoretically exist.

The Copyright Society of Nigeria — COSON — is the collective management organisation authorised to collect and distribute performance royalties on behalf of Nigerian musicians. When your music is played on radio, performed in public, streamed through platforms, or used in advertising, royalties should flow through COSON to you. The practical reality is that COSON's collection efficiency is limited, particularly for smaller and newer artists, and the distribution of collected royalties has been controversial. Nevertheless, registering with COSON is the essential first step for any Nigerian artist seeking to capture performance income — artists who are not registered cannot receive distributions regardless of how widely their music is used.

International distribution platforms including DistroKid, TuneCore, and CD Baby not only distribute music globally but also register compositions with international performance rights organisations — organisations like ASCAP, BMI, and PRS — that collect royalties when Nigerian music is performed or streamed internationally. This registration, which happens automatically through the distribution process, is how Nigerian artists can capture the streaming and performance income that their music generates in markets from the United States to South Korea without any additional administrative effort.

The contractual dimension of music copyright is where the most significant losses occur for Nigerian artists. Record deals, production agreements, and publishing contracts that transfer copyright ownership to labels and publishers in exchange for upfront payments or services have cost Nigerian artists tens of billions of naira in future royalty income. Every Nigerian artist should have a music lawyer review any agreement before signing, understand exactly what rights they are transferring and for how long, and ensure that reversion clauses — provisions allowing rights to return to the artist if the label fails to actively exploit them — are included. The music business is a contractual business. Artists who understand the contracts they sign build careers. Those who do not fund other people's retirements.

💡 Key Takeaway: Register with COSON immediately, document all your original work with timestamps, use distribution platforms that register publishing rights automatically, and never release music without a written agreement covering ownership.
