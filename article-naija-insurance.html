Insurance in Nigeria: Why You Need It and Which Policies Are Actually Worth Buying

Most Nigerians have no insurance of any kind. One accident, illness, or fire can wipe out everything they have built.

✍ NaijaBuzz Desk•May 2026•6 min read

Insurance is one of the most consistently undervalued financial tools available to Nigerians, and its absence from most household financial plans creates a vulnerability that a single bad event can exploit catastrophically. A road accident that requires surgery. A fire that destroys a shop. A sudden death that leaves dependants with no income. An illness that requires hospitalisation for weeks. Any of these events, without insurance, can wipe out years of savings, push a family into debt, or destroy a business that took a decade to build.

The penetration of insurance in Nigeria is among the lowest in the world — less than 2% of the population has any form of insurance coverage, compared to over 80% in developed economies. The reasons are partly cultural — a widespread belief that insurance is a scam, that paying for something you may never use is a waste — and partly practical, as many Nigerians have genuinely had negative experiences with insurance companies that disputed claims or made payout processes impossibly difficult.

Health insurance is the most urgently needed product for most Nigerians. Medical emergencies are unpredictable, healthcare costs are rising rapidly, and the out-of-pocket costs of hospitalisation or surgery can reach ₦500,000 to ₦5 million in a single episode. The National Health Insurance Scheme provides subsidised coverage for formal sector employees, and several private Health Maintenance Organisations offer individual and family plans starting from ₦15,000 to ₦50,000 annually. Companies like Reliance HMO, Hygeia, and Avon HMO have built reputations for relatively straightforward claims processes.

Life insurance matters most for anyone with financial dependants — a spouse, children, parents, or siblings who rely on your income. A basic term life policy that pays out a lump sum to your beneficiaries if you die costs between ₦5,000 and ₦20,000 annually for coverage of ₦2 million to ₦10 million. This is not an investment — term life pays nothing if you survive the policy period — but it is the cheapest way to ensure that your death does not also destroy the financial security of everyone who depends on you.

Motor insurance is legally mandatory in Nigeria yet widely ignored. Beyond the legal obligation, comprehensive motor insurance protects against the potentially enormous costs of accidents — both damage to your own vehicle and liability for damage or injury to third parties. The calculation is simple: a serious accident can cost more than the annual premium of a comprehensive policy in a single day. For businesses, property insurance — covering stock, equipment, and physical premises against fire, theft, and flood — is the policy that most often separates businesses that survive disasters from those that do not. Insurance is not a luxury. For anyone with assets, income, or dependants, it is a necessity.

📍 The Real Lesson: Health insurance and life insurance are the two most important policies for every Nigerian. Start with the NHIS or an HMO plan and a basic life policy — together they cost less than most people spend on data.
