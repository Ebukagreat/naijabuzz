High Blood Pressure: Why It Is Killing Nigerians Silently and What to Do About It

Hypertension affects one in three Nigerian adults yet most are undiagnosed. It is Nigeria's deadliest silent killer.

✍ NaijaBuzz Editorial•May 2026•6 min read

It is called the silent killer for a reason. High blood pressure — hypertension — typically produces no symptoms until it has already caused serious damage to the heart, brain, or kidneys. By the time a Nigerian discovers they have hypertension through a stroke, heart attack, or kidney failure, the disease has often been silently progressing for years. The World Health Organization estimates that hypertension affects approximately one in three Nigerian adults — roughly 35 million people — yet the majority have never been diagnosed.

The risk factors driving Nigeria's hypertension epidemic are a convergence of diet, lifestyle, and genetics. Nigerian cuisine is typically high in sodium — from salt, seasoning cubes, smoked fish, and fermented condiments — and this dietary pattern significantly elevates blood pressure over time. Physical inactivity, driven by sedentary office work and long commutes, removes one of the most effective natural blood pressure regulators available. Chronic stress — financial pressure, job insecurity, family obligations — activates hormonal pathways that raise blood pressure. And West African genetic heritage carries a higher baseline predisposition to hypertension than many other populations.

The consequences of untreated hypertension are devastating. Stroke is the leading cause of death among Nigerian adults, and hypertension is its primary driver. Chronic kidney disease, which is reaching epidemic proportions in Nigeria, is strongly linked to uncontrolled blood pressure. Heart failure, which leaves patients dependent on expensive medications and frequent hospitalisations, is a direct consequence of the cardiac strain that sustained hypertension creates. And all of these outcomes are largely preventable with diagnosis and appropriate management.

The practical steps every Nigerian should take are simple. First, know your number — blood pressure machines are available at most pharmacies and healthcare centres for a few hundred naira, and many churches and community organisations offer free screening. A normal blood pressure is below 120/80 mmHg. Readings consistently above 140/90 mmHg require medical attention. Second, reduce dietary sodium by cutting back on seasoning cubes, using less table salt, and reducing smoked and processed foods. Third, exercise — even brisk walking for 30 minutes five times per week has a clinically meaningful blood pressure lowering effect. And if a doctor prescribes medication, take it consistently rather than stopping when you feel better. Hypertension is a chronic condition, not an acute one, and the medication manages it rather than curing it.

📍 Bottom Line: Check your blood pressure today. Reduce salt, exercise regularly, manage stress, and take medication consistently if prescribed. Hypertension is manageable — but only if you know you have it.
