Kidney Disease in Nigeria: The Silent Epidemic and How to Protect Your Kidneys

Chronic kidney disease is at epidemic levels in Nigeria with most patients unaware until the disease is advanced. Here is how to protect yourself.

✍ NaijaBuzz Staff•May 2026•6 min read

The kidneys are among the most remarkable organs in the human body — filtering approximately 200 litres of blood daily, removing waste products, regulating fluid balance, controlling blood pressure, and producing hormones that stimulate red blood cell production. They perform this extraordinary work silently and continuously, without any sensation or signal that would alert us to their functioning — or their deterioration. This silence is precisely what makes chronic kidney disease so dangerous: by the time most Nigerian patients experience symptoms, 60 to 70% of kidney function may already be permanently lost.

Nigeria faces a chronic kidney disease epidemic of alarming proportions. Studies suggest that between 8 and 12% of Nigerian adults — somewhere between 17 and 26 million people — have some degree of chronic kidney disease. The majority are undiagnosed. The major risk factors driving this epidemic are hypertension and diabetes — both of which are themselves at epidemic levels in Nigeria and both of which directly damage the small blood vessels and filtering units of the kidneys over time. Uncontrolled high blood pressure is the most common cause of kidney failure in Nigeria.

Traditional herbal medicines and unprescribed pharmaceutical drugs are significant and underappreciated contributors to kidney damage in Nigeria. Many herbal preparations contain compounds that are directly toxic to kidney tissue. Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs — painkillers including ibuprofen, diclofenac, and the various analgesic combinations sold over the counter in Nigerian pharmacies — cause kidney damage when taken regularly or in high doses, particularly in people who are already dehydrated or have existing kidney vulnerability. The cultural normalisation of self-medication in Nigeria makes this risk particularly acute.

The consequences of kidney failure in Nigeria are devastating. Dialysis — the artificial kidney filtering treatment that sustains life when kidneys fail — costs between ₦80,000 and ₦150,000 per month, three sessions weekly. The vast majority of Nigerian kidney failure patients cannot afford this. Kidney transplantation, which offers the best long-term outcome, is available at only a handful of Nigerian centres at costs that are prohibitive for most families. The result is that kidney failure in Nigeria is, for most patients, a death sentence — preventable if identified early, fatal when discovered late.

Prevention and early detection are the only realistic strategies available. Every Nigerian adult with hypertension or diabetes should have annual kidney function tests — serum creatinine and urine protein — to monitor for early deterioration. Staying well hydrated, maintaining blood pressure below 130/80 mmHg, controlling blood sugar in diabetics, avoiding unprescribed painkillers, being cautious with herbal medicines of unknown composition, and maintaining a healthy weight are the evidence-based interventions that protect kidneys. None of them are expensive. All of them are achievable. And the kidneys they protect are irreplaceable.

💡 Key Takeaway: Control your blood pressure and blood sugar, stay well hydrated, avoid excessive painkillers, and get a kidney function test at your next medical checkup. Early detection makes kidney disease manageable.
