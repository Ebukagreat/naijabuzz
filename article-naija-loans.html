Loans in Nigeria in 2026: Which Ones to Take, Which to Avoid, and How to Borrow Smartly

Nigerian lending options have multiplied dramatically. Here is how to borrow smartly and avoid the loan traps that are destroying Nigerian finances.

✍ NaijaBuzz Staff•May 2026•6 min read

The Nigerian lending landscape has been transformed by fintech in the past five years. Where accessing a loan previously required lengthy bank processes, collateral that most Nigerians do not have, and relationships that most cannot leverage, mobile lending apps have made credit available to millions of Nigerians with nothing more than a smartphone, a BVN, and a bank account. This democratisation of credit is genuinely positive for economic inclusion. But it has also introduced a new set of financial traps that are causing serious harm to Nigerian borrowers who do not understand what they are agreeing to.

The most important concept for any Nigerian considering a loan is the Annual Percentage Rate — the true annual cost of the loan expressed as a percentage. Many Nigerian loan apps advertise daily or monthly interest rates that appear modest but translate to devastating APRs. A loan app advertising 5% monthly interest is charging 60% APR. A 2% daily rate — offered by some short-term lending apps — translates to 730% APR. For comparison, Nigerian bank overdraft rates of 25 to 30% annually, while high, are significantly cheaper than most digital loan products. Understanding the true annual cost of any loan before accepting it is non-negotiable.

The contact-harvesting loan apps that proliferated in Nigeria between 2020 and 2024 — apps that require access to your phone contacts as part of the loan agreement and then send shame messages to those contacts when repayments are missed — represent a predatory lending category that has caused genuine psychological harm to Nigerian borrowers. The National Information Technology Development Agency and the Federal Competition and Consumer Protection Commission have taken action against some of these operators, and the practice has become less common but not eliminated. Any loan app that requests access to your contacts as a condition of borrowing should be rejected immediately.

The loan products most worth considering for Nigerians with legitimate borrowing needs are salary advance products offered through your employer or payroll platforms — which typically charge modest fees rather than interest, and are repaid directly from salary before it reaches the employee. Credit products from regulated banks and microfinance institutions, while requiring more documentation, offer much more favourable terms than digital lending apps. And the Federal Government's various intervention funds — the Trader Moni, Market Moni, and similar programmes — offer near-zero interest loans to small business owners who qualify.

The fundamental rule of smart borrowing applies regardless of the lender or product: only borrow money that will generate returns greater than the cost of the loan. Borrowing to buy a productive asset, fund a profitable business order, or address a genuine emergency that would cost more to ignore is rational. Borrowing to fund consumption — buying clothes, phones, entertainment, or lifestyle spending that your income cannot currently support — is a path to compounding financial distress. Nigeria's lending industry has made credit accessible. Whether that access improves or damages your financial position depends entirely on whether you use it wisely.

💡 Key Takeaway: Only borrow for income-generating purposes or genuine emergencies. Know your total repayment amount before signing anything, avoid loan apps that access your contacts, and never borrow from one lender to repay another.
