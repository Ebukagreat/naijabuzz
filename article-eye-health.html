Eye Health in Nigeria: Why Millions Are Going Blind Unnecessarily and How to Protect Your Vision

Nigeria has over 1 million blind people and millions more with preventable visual impairment. Here is how to protect your eyesight.

✍ NaijaBuzz Staff•May 2026•5 min read

Nigeria has one of the highest burdens of blindness and visual impairment in sub-Saharan Africa, with an estimated 1 million people who are blind and over 4 million with significant visual impairment — the majority of whom have conditions that are either preventable or treatable with available medical interventions. The tragedy is not primarily medical but logistical and behavioural: the knowledge and technology to prevent most Nigerian blindness exists, but the healthcare system capacity to deliver it and the public awareness to seek it are both critically insufficient.

Cataract — the progressive clouding of the eye's natural lens that causes gradually worsening vision and eventual blindness — is the leading cause of blindness in Nigeria, accounting for over 40% of cases. It is entirely treatable through a surgical procedure that costs between ₦30,000 and ₦150,000 at quality facilities and restores functional vision in the vast majority of patients. Yet hundreds of thousands of Nigerians are blind from cataracts simply because they have not accessed or cannot afford the surgical correction. Regular eye examinations that detect cataracts in their early stages allow intervention before vision loss is severe.

Glaucoma — a condition in which elevated pressure within the eye gradually damages the optic nerve, causing irreversible peripheral vision loss that progresses to complete blindness if untreated — is the second major cause of blindness in Nigeria. Unlike cataracts, glaucoma causes no pain or obvious symptoms until vision loss is advanced. By the time most Nigerian glaucoma patients are diagnosed, significant permanent damage has already occurred. The only way to catch glaucoma early enough to preserve vision is through regular comprehensive eye examinations that include measurement of intraocular pressure. Anyone with a family history of glaucoma — which significantly elevates personal risk — should have annual eye pressure checks from age 35 onwards.

Diabetic retinopathy — damage to the blood vessels of the retina caused by poorly controlled blood sugar in diabetic patients — is a growing cause of preventable blindness in Nigeria as the diabetes epidemic expands. In its early stages, diabetic retinopathy causes no symptoms but is detectable through retinal examination. Regular eye examinations for all diabetic patients — at least annually — allow laser treatment that halts progression before vision loss occurs. This preventive intervention is available in Nigeria but chronically underutilised because many diabetic patients are not aware of their risk.

The practical steps every Nigerian can take to protect their vision are straightforward. Wearing sunglasses with UV protection when outdoors in Nigerian sunshine reduces cumulative UV damage that contributes to cataract formation. Taking regular screen breaks — the 20-20-20 rule of looking at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds every 20 minutes — reduces the digital eye strain that causes headaches, blurred vision, and long-term discomfort for the growing proportion of Nigerians who spend significant time on screens. Eating a diet rich in dark leafy vegetables — ugwu, waterleaf, bitter leaf — provides the lutein and zeaxanthin that research associates with reduced risk of age-related macular degeneration. And scheduling a comprehensive eye examination every two years — rather than waiting until symptoms appear — is the single most important protective action available.

💡 Key Takeaway: Get a comprehensive eye examination every two years, wear sunglasses with UV protection outdoors, reduce screen exposure, and never ignore sudden changes in vision — they can signal medical emergencies.
