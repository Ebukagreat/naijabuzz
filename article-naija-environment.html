Plastic Pollution in Nigeria: The Crisis Choking Our Cities and Waterways

Nigeria is one of the world's largest contributors to ocean plastic pollution. Here is the full story and what individuals and businesses can do.

✍ NaijaBuzz Staff•June 2026•5 min read

Lagos generates an estimated 13,000 tonnes of solid waste daily, of which a significant and growing proportion is plastic — sachet water packets, plastic bags, PET bottles, food packaging, and the accumulated output of a consumer economy that has embraced plastic convenience without developing the waste management infrastructure to handle its consequences. Nigerian waterways, from the Lagos Lagoon to the creeks of the Niger Delta to the rivers of the Middle Belt, are increasingly choked with plastic waste that has entered the water system through inadequate disposal, flooding, and the absence of functional waste collection in many communities.

The sachet water industry — which provides the primary source of drinking water for the majority of urban Nigerians who cannot access clean tap water — is the single largest source of low-value plastic waste in the country. Hundreds of millions of 500ml sachets are consumed daily across Nigeria, each producing a tiny piece of low-density polyethylene plastic that has essentially no recycling value and an environmental persistence of hundreds of years. The industry exists because public water infrastructure has failed Nigerian citizens — sachet water is not a cause of the plastic pollution problem but a symptom of the deeper infrastructure failure that created it.

Nigeria ratified the Basel Convention on hazardous waste management and has developed national plastic waste management policies that, on paper, create a framework for addressing the crisis. The practical implementation gap is enormous — waste collection rates in most Nigerian cities are below 50%, recycling infrastructure is rudimentary, and enforcement of existing environmental regulations is inconsistent at best. The private sector has filled some gaps: recycling companies including Wecyclers, Pakam, and several others pay Nigerians for sorted plastic waste, creating income incentives for informal waste collectors and reducing the volume of plastic entering waterways.

The business opportunity in Nigerian waste management is genuinely significant. The plastics that litter Nigerian streets and clog waterways represent raw material for recycled plastic products — building materials, furniture, agricultural inputs — that have real market value. Entrepreneurs who build collection, sorting, and processing infrastructure at scale can simultaneously address an environmental problem and build profitable businesses. International sustainability-focused investors are increasingly interested in African waste management opportunities, providing capital access that was previously unavailable.

For individual Nigerians, the practical actions that reduce plastic pollution are accessible and meaningful in aggregate. Carrying a reusable water bottle rather than buying sachet water reduces daily plastic consumption significantly. Refusing single-use plastic bags at markets and shops and bringing reusable bags instead removes plastic from the waste stream before it enters it. Sorting recyclable plastics and selling or donating them to recycling collectors like Wecyclers provides income while reducing environmental harm. And supporting businesses that use sustainable packaging over those that default to single-use plastic creates market pressure for better packaging practices. Nigeria's plastic crisis was created by the aggregate of millions of individual choices. It will be resolved the same way.

✅ Worth Remembering: Reduce single-use plastic in your daily life, support businesses using sustainable packaging, and advocate for effective waste management policy. Individual choices aggregated across millions of Nigerians make a real difference.
