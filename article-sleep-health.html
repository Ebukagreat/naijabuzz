Why Nigerians Are Not Getting Enough Sleep and the Serious Health Consequences

Studies show most Nigerians sleep fewer than six hours per night. The health consequences are more serious than most people realise.

✍ NaijaBuzz Staff•May 2026•5 min read

In Nigerian culture, sleeping long hours has traditionally been associated with laziness. The person who wakes at 5am and works until midnight is celebrated; the one who prioritises eight hours of sleep is suspect. This cultural attitude toward sleep — combined with the genuine demands of Nigerian urban life, including long commutes, multiple income streams, and noisy environments — has created a nation of chronically sleep-deprived people paying a health price they largely do not recognise.

Research conducted across Nigerian cities consistently finds that the majority of urban adults sleep fewer than six hours per night. The recommended minimum for adults is seven to nine hours. This gap — seemingly small in isolation — compounds into catastrophic health consequences over time. Chronic sleep deprivation has been scientifically linked to increased risk of heart disease, type 2 diabetes, obesity, depression, anxiety, impaired immune function, and reduced cognitive performance. It also dramatically increases the risk of accidents — both vehicular and occupational.

The specific drivers of poor sleep in Nigeria include noise pollution from generators — which run throughout the night in most urban households — heat from inadequate or non-existent air conditioning, the blue light emitted by smartphones used late into the night, irregular work schedules, and the genuine financial anxiety that keeps many Nigerians lying awake calculating their expenses. Some of these are structural problems that individuals cannot easily solve. But many can be meaningfully addressed with relatively simple interventions.

The most evidence-based sleep improvements available to Nigerians are: stopping screen use — phone, laptop, television — at least one hour before the intended sleep time, as the blue light from screens suppresses melatonin production. Keeping the sleep environment as dark and cool as possible, even if this means investing in a basic fan or blackout curtain. Maintaining a consistent sleep and wake time even on weekends, which regulates the body's internal clock. And avoiding caffeine — including the heavily caffeinated energy drinks that have become popular in Nigeria — after 2pm.

Sleep is not a passive state. It is when your brain consolidates memories, your body repairs tissue, your immune system resets, and your hormones rebalance. Every hour of sleep you sacrifice to scroll social media or watch an extra episode is an hour stolen from your health, your productivity, and your longevity. In a country where the cost of healthcare is prohibitive and the burden of illness falls entirely on individuals and families, sleep is one of the most cost-effective health investments available. It costs nothing. It delivers everything.

💡 Key Takeaway: Prioritise 7 to 8 hours of sleep every night. Turn off screens an hour before bed, keep your room cool and dark, and treat sleep as a health investment, not a luxury.
