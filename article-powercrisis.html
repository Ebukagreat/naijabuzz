Nigeria's Power Crisis: Why the Lights Keep Going Out and What Can Fix It

Nigeria has gas, dams, and sunshine. Yet most households receive fewer than 10 hours of electricity daily. Here is the honest analysis.

✍ NaijaBuzz Editorial•May 2026•7 min read

Nigeria is sitting on some of the world's largest natural gas reserves, has multiple large hydroelectric dams, and receives intense sunshine 300 days a year. Yet the average Nigerian household receives fewer than 10 hours of electricity per day, and many receive far less. This is not a resource problem. It is a governance, infrastructure, and investment failure of staggering proportions — one that costs the Nigerian economy an estimated $29 billion annually.

The core problems are well understood. Nigeria's gas-fired power plants — which generate the majority of the country's electricity — are chronically underfueled because the pipelines supplying them are old, vandalised, and poorly maintained. The transmission grid, managed by the Transmission Company of Nigeria, can only carry a fraction of the electricity that could theoretically be generated because its cables and transformers have not been meaningfully upgraded in decades. And the distribution companies, privatised in 2013 with great fanfare, have largely failed to invest in improving the last-mile infrastructure that actually delivers electricity to homes and businesses.

The solutions are neither secret nor technically complex. Massive investment in gas pipeline security and capacity would allow existing power plants to run at full capacity. A transmission grid upgrade — estimated to cost between $3 and $5 billion — would allow far more electricity to reach consumers. And regulatory reforms that allow electricity pricing to reflect actual costs while protecting the poorest consumers would attract the private investment needed to build new generation capacity.

In the meantime, solar energy is emerging as Nigeria's most practical short-term solution. The cost of solar panels has fallen by over 80% in the past decade, and off-grid solar systems now provide reliable electricity to millions of Nigerian households and businesses that have given up waiting for the grid. Companies like Lumos, Rensource, and Rural Spark are building the distributed solar infrastructure that the government has failed to deliver. The lights may yet come on in Nigeria — just not necessarily through the switch on the wall.

📍 Bottom Line: Off-grid solar is Nigeria's most practical electricity solution today. Companies like Lumos and Rensource are leading the way forward.
