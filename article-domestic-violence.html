Domestic Violence in Nigeria: Breaking the Silence and Finding Help

Domestic violence is one of Nigeria's most common and least discussed health and safety crises. Here is what every Nigerian needs to know.

✍ NaijaBuzz Editorial•May 2026•5 min read

Domestic violence — physical, sexual, emotional, and economic abuse within intimate partner relationships and family contexts — is one of Nigeria's most pervasive and least acknowledged public health crises. The National Demographic and Health Survey consistently finds that over 30% of Nigerian women have experienced physical violence at the hands of an intimate partner, and that the majority of these incidents are never reported to authorities. Cultural norms that frame domestic violence as a private family matter, religious teachings that emphasise wifely submission and marital permanence above personal safety, and a legal system that has historically failed to protect victims have combined to create an environment in which abuse is widespread and largely invisible.

The Violence Against Persons Prohibition Act, passed federally in 2015 and subsequently adopted by many states, provides the legal framework for prosecuting domestic violence in Nigeria. The Act criminalises physical, sexual, psychological, and economic abuse within domestic relationships, establishes protection orders that courts can issue to restrain abusers, and creates offences including female genital mutilation, forced marriage, and harmful widowhood practices. Many Nigerians — including many law enforcement officers — are unaware of the Act's provisions, which means that victims who know their legal rights are better positioned to access the protection it offers.

The barriers that prevent Nigerian domestic violence victims from seeking help are multiple and real. Economic dependence on an abusive partner — particularly for women without independent income or assets — makes leaving feel financially impossible. Fear of social stigma and family pressure to maintain the marriage regardless of safety conditions silences many victims who might otherwise seek help. Children, and the genuine concern that leaving will harm them or separate them from their mother, is a factor that keeps many women in dangerous situations. And the genuine inadequacy of shelter and support infrastructure in Nigeria means that victims who do leave often have nowhere safe to go.

The organisations providing support to Nigerian domestic violence victims, despite inadequate government funding and overwhelming demand, are doing critically important work. WARIF — Women at Risk International Foundation — operates a helpline at 08000720720 and provides medical, psychosocial, and legal support to survivors of gender-based violence in Lagos. Project Alert on Violence Against Women operates a shelter and counselling services for abused women. And NAPTIP — the National Agency for the Prohibition of Trafficking in Persons — can be reached at 0800-NAPTIP-0 and deals with cases involving trafficking and severe abuse. These organisations need more resources and greater public awareness of their services.

For Nigerians who suspect that a friend, family member, or colleague is experiencing domestic violence, the most helpful response is neither silence nor pressure but a gentle, consistent offer of non-judgmental support. Telling someone experiencing abuse that you are there for them, that you believe them, that what is happening to them is not acceptable and not their fault, and that you will support them whatever they decide to do — without pressuring them toward a specific action before they are ready — provides the foundation of trust from which they can eventually seek help on their own timeline. Domestic violence ends when communities refuse to normalise it. That refusal begins with each of us.

📌 In Practice: Domestic violence is never acceptable and never the victim's fault. If you or someone you know is experiencing abuse, contact the WARIF helpline on 08000720720 or the NAPTIP hotline on 0800-NAPTIP-0. Help is available.
