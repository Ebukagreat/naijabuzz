Protein in the Nigerian Diet: Are You Getting Enough and Which Sources Are Best

Protein deficiency is more common in Nigeria than most people realise. Here is how to get enough from foods you already know and love.

✍ NaijaBuzz Staff•June 2026•5 min read

Protein is the most essential macronutrient for muscle maintenance, immune function, hormone production, and tissue repair — and it is the nutrient most commonly deficient in Nigerian diets, particularly among children, pregnant women, and elderly adults. The irony is that Nigeria has abundant, affordable protein sources that have fed its population healthily for generations, yet changing food habits, urbanisation, and the cultural prestige attached to carbohydrate-heavy meals has progressively reduced protein intake across demographic groups.

The daily protein requirement for an average adult is approximately 0.8 grams per kilogram of body weight — meaning a 70-kilogram Nigerian needs roughly 56 grams of protein daily. For context, a medium-sized egg contains 6 grams of protein, a cup of cooked beans contains 15 grams, a 100-gram piece of mackerel contains 20 grams, and a handful of groundnuts contains 7 grams. Achieving the daily requirement is entirely possible from affordable Nigerian foods — the challenge is understanding which meals provide adequate protein and structuring eating patterns accordingly.

Beans — in all their Nigerian varieties including honey beans, black-eyed peas, and brown beans — are Nigeria's most valuable and underutilised protein source. They are cheap, widely available, filling, and contain not only protein but significant fibre, iron, and folate. The cultural association of beans with poverty — the sense that eating beans regularly is a sign of financial inability to afford meat — has reduced bean consumption among upwardly mobile Nigerians in ways that are nutritionally counterproductive. A well-prepared plate of beans and plantain with palm oil provides more complete nutrition than a plate of white rice with a thin piece of chicken, at lower cost.

Fish — particularly the catfish, tilapia, mackerel, and stockfish widely available across Nigeria — provides complete protein with all essential amino acids, plus omega-3 fatty acids that support brain and cardiovascular health. The traditional Nigerian pattern of using fish as a soup ingredient rather than as a main protein source reduces the protein contribution of each meal; increasing portion sizes of fish relative to carbohydrates in daily meals significantly improves overall protein intake without requiring expensive imports or supplements. Crayfish and dried fish, used in almost every Nigerian soup, provide meaningful additional protein at minimal cost.

Eggs remain one of the most nutrient-dense and cost-effective foods available to Nigerians. The historical concern about cholesterol from egg yolks — which drove recommendations to limit egg consumption — has been substantially revised by research showing that dietary cholesterol from whole foods has minimal impact on blood cholesterol for most people. Two eggs for breakfast provide 12 grams of high-quality protein, significant B vitamins, vitamin D, and choline at a cost of ₦400 to ₦600. For Nigerian households trying to improve nutritional quality without increasing food expenditure, replacing processed breakfast options — instant noodles, bread and margarine — with eggs is one of the highest-value dietary changes available.

💡 Key Takeaway: Beans, eggs, fish, and groundnuts are Nigeria's most affordable complete protein sources. Include at least one at every meal to meet daily protein requirements without expensive supplements.
